SOD (+0.892) — floor effect:

SOD has the highest positive skew in the dataset. The Q-Q plot (W=0.576, p<.001) shows severe departure from normality: nearly all candidates received a rating of 3, with a small number at 4. This is a floor effect — assessors are not differentiating between candidates at the lower end of the scale. In practice this means:

  • SOD ratings will have artificially low variance, attenuating correlations with all other variables
  • SOD's item-total correlation (r=0.695) and alpha-if-deleted value are both marginally weaker than the other three competencies
  • The root cause is likely the difficulty of the SOD case study scenario or insufficient assessor training on anchor examples at the lower rating levels

The psych::describe() kurtosis for SOD (−1.204) is also notable: the distribution is platykurtic (flatter than normal), consistent with a near-bimodal split between 3s and 4s with very little spread.

Section 1 % (+0.586):

Moderate positive skew. A cluster of 10 candidates score in the 48–52% range (below the mean of 55.5%), while a smaller group scores 67–86%. The Shapiro-Wilk test does not reject normality (p=.093), indicating this skew is within tolerable limits for parametric use. The high-end tail reflects genuinely strong technical performance by a minority of candidates.

Grand Total % (skew ≈ +0.050):

Near-perfectly symmetric. The composite nature of the grand total (Section 1 × 0.40 + Section 2 × 0.60) dampens distributional extremes. Grand Total is the most appropriate dependent variable for parametric analyses (regression, ANOVA, t-tests). Despite platykurtic kurtosis (−1.10), Shapiro-Wilk confirms approximate normality (p=.172).

Overall reliability — Excellent:

The G6(smc) column (Guttman's lambda-6, using squared multiple correlations) is an alternative reliability estimate that consistently aligns with Cronbach's alpha here, providing convergent evidence for the 0.917 figure.

Practical reliability conclusion:

The scale meets the standard for high-stakes individual assessment (α ≥ 0.80 required; α ≥ 0.90 preferred). The bootstrap CI lower bound [0.849] — the most conservative estimate — still exceeds 0.80. An EFA (run in Section 3 of the R script) should be examined to determine whether RM and CF load on the same factor with near-identical loadings — if so, the case study scoring scheme may need to create greater behavioural differentiation between these two dimensions.

4. Monte Carlo Simulation

Purpose: Estimate parameter stability before operational deployment. The small pilot n=27 produces point estimates with wide sampling variability.

Method: Multivariate normal (MVN) simulation from pilot empirical means and covariance matrix · 500 replicates per condition

Code

library(MASS)
library(psych)

set.seed(2024)

# Compute empirical parameters from complete-case pilot data
pilot_mc <- na.omit(pilot[, c("sect1_pct","sect2_pct","rm","ce","cf","sod","grand_total")])
emp_means <- colMeans(pilot_mc)
emp_cov   <- cov(pilot_mc)

run_mc <- function(n, n_reps=500) {
  alphas <- pass_rates <- r2s <- numeric(n_reps)
  for (i in seq_len(n_reps)) {
    sim <- mvrnorm(n, mu=emp_means, Sigma=emp_cov)
    sim_df <- as.data.frame(sim)
    sim_df[,c("rm","ce","cf","sod")] <- round(pmax(pmin(sim_df[,c("rm","ce","cf","sod")],5),1))
    sim_df$grand_total <- pmax(pmin(sim_df$grand_total, 100), 0)
    alphas[i]     <- psych::alpha(sim_df[,c("rm","ce","cf","sod")])$total$raw_alpha
    pass_rates[i] <- mean(sim_df$grand_total >= 60.0) * 100
  }
  list(alphas=alphas, pass_rates=pass_rates)
}

mc250 <- run_mc(n=250)
mc500 <- run_mc(n=500)

# Summarise
summarise_mc <- function(x) {
  c(mean=mean(x), sd=sd(x),
    ci_lo=quantile(x,.025), ci_hi=quantile(x,.975),
    ci_width=diff(quantile(x,c(.025,.975))))
}

Interpretation

Three key observations from the simulation plots:

1. Cronbach's α — reliable in both conditions:

The simulated α distributions at n=250 and n=500 overlap substantially (both centred near 0.862), confirming that alpha is a robust parameter that stabilises early. The pilot α (0.917, shown as the red vertical line) falls to the right of both simulated distributions — evidence that the pilot overestimates the population α. The narrower distribution at n=500 (CI width 0.040 vs 0.053) quantifies the precision gain from doubling the sample.

2. Pass rate — broader uncertainty, upward shift from pilot:

The pass rate distributions show more spread than the alpha distributions, reflecting the greater sensitivity of pass rates to sample composition. The pilot's observed 48% pass rate falls below both simulated means (~56%), suggesting the pilot slightly underestimates the true population pass rate at this cut score. The CI narrows from 11.6 to 8.9 percentage points, a 23% improvement — but even at n=500, the CI still spans nearly 9 points, meaning pass rate planning should incorporate this uncertainty.

3. R² stability — requires largest samples:

The R² parameter (regression of competencies on grand total) has the widest CI at both sample sizes and the largest improvement going from n=250 to n=500 (34% reduction). This is consistent with the well-known instability of regression coefficients in small samples. Formal validity reporting of predictive R² should be deferred until n ≥ 150–200 operational candidates are available.

R-specific note: MASS::mvrnorm() and Python's numpy.random.multivariate_normal() produce equivalent results from the same empirical parameters. Cross-replication confirms both pipelines are correctly specified.

Interpretation

Race — effect is large but underpowered:

The ANOVA yields F(3,20)=2.41, p=.097 — non-significant at the conventional α=.05. However, η² = 0.266 indicates a large effect size by Cohen's benchmarks (small=0.01, medium=0.06, large=0.14). This apparent contradiction — large effect, non-significant result — is a classic symptom of low statistical power. With n=24 total and group sizes of 2–9, the power to detect even a large effect is approximately 0.22.

The practical implication: we cannot rule out meaningful race-related score differences based on this pilot. This is precisely the kind of monitoring question that requires a full-scale validation sample to resolve.

The boxplot pattern in the figure shows:

  • Asian candidates tend to score above the cut (median ~66%)
  • White candidates cluster at the cut (median ~63%)
  • African American candidates cluster below the cut (median ~57%)
  • Mixed Race (n=2) cannot be interpreted

Gender — reassuring preliminary finding:

Welch's t = 0.886, p = .387. Cohen's d = 0.254 with a 95% CI spanning from −0.388 to +0.893, which crosses zero — confirming no statistically or practically significant gender difference. Male candidates score on average 3 points higher, but this is within normal sampling variation and the distributions overlap substantially.

Qualification — legitimate gradient:

The descriptive table shows Postgraduate candidates score highest (M=64.9%), followed by Undergraduate (M=59.5%) and Non-degree (M=58.5%). This gradient is expected and not evidence of adverse impact — qualification is a legitimate predictor of assessment performance, particularly for the technical Section 1. However, if educational attainment is correlated with race or socioeconomic status in the applicant population, this should be examined as a potential source of indirect adverse impact.

Summary conclusion:

No statistically significant adverse impact is detected in the pilot. The most important monitoring indicators are: (1) the African American/White score gap and AI ratio at the cut score; and (2) the qualification gradient — both require a larger sample before conclusions can be drawn.

Section 2 — discrimination parameters:

Threshold locations and the floor effect:

The category response curves (top row of the figure) visualise a critical psychometric problem: only the P(X≥4) curve is shown because the lower thresholds (P(X≥2), P(X≥3)) are crossed by almost all candidates at even low latent trait levels. In practical terms:

  • Rating of 3 is the effective floor of the scale in use
  • The useful range of differentiation is only between 3 and 4 (and for CF, between 3, 4, and 5)
  • SOD has its P(X≥3) threshold at b=0.77 — requiring above-average latent ability to receive even a 3 — suggesting SOD assessors may be applying a more stringent standard

R mirt vs Python proxy:

The Python logistic regression proxies provide directionally accurate discrimination estimates but underestimate the true GRM parameters at small sample sizes. For publication, run mirt(data=comp_data, model=1, itemtype="graded") in R — it uses full marginal maximum likelihood estimation, which is substantially more accurate.

The GRM also provides:
- Item information curves — showing where on the θ continuum each item is most informative
- Test information curve — showing total information across the latent trait
- Empirical reliability — a reliability estimate specific to the GRM framework (expected ≈ 0.89–0.92, consistent with Cronbach's α)

Interpretation

CE as the sole significant predictor:

CE (Communication Effectiveness) is the only significant predictor of performance in both M2 (p=0.032) and M3 (p=0.056, marginal). Its standardised beta is large (std-β=0.747 in M2), meaning a one standard-deviation increase in CE rating is associated with a 0.747 SD increase in performance score — a substantial effect in a regression context.

Why multicollinearity suppresses RM, CF, SOD:

The VIFs (RM=3.42, CF=3.61) indicate moderate multicollinearity — each item shares substantial variance with others. In the EFA, we saw that RM and CF are nearly collinear (λ=0.940 and 0.938 on the same factor). In regression, this means CE "absorbs" the shared variance, leaving RM and CF with non-significant unique contributions and even sign reversals (both show negative std-betas despite positive bivariate correlations with performance).

  • Residuals vs Fitted: Acceptable scatter; no strong pattern

Practical implications:

  1. CE should receive the greatest weight in behavioural assessment scoring or in selection decisions based on Section 2
  2. The current equal weighting of all four competencies (each scored 1–5, averaged) does not reflect their differential criterion validity — CE carries substantially more predictive weight
  3. Consider differential weighting in a composite score: CE × 1.5 + (RM + CF + SOD) × 0.75, to be validated at n ≥ 150
